If you have a baby with reflux, the mere mention of "tummy time" probably makes you break into a sweat. You lay your sweet baby down, and within thirty seconds, they are crying, squirming, and spitting up their entire last meal all over the clean play mat. You feel terrible for causing them pain, and you're frustrated because you *know* tummy time is important, but it just feels impossible right now.

First, take a deep breath. You are doing a great job, and your baby is okay. As a pediatric occupational therapist (and a mom who has been in the trenches with a very uncomfortable, spit-up-prone baby), I want to validate how hard this is. Tummy time with a reflux baby is a completely different ballgame.

But here’s the good news: we can modify it. You don't have to push through the screaming, and your baby can still get the developmental benefits they need. Let's talk about why it's so hard, why they still need it, and how we can make it better.

## Why is Tummy Time Harder for Reflux Babies?

To understand why your baby hates tummy time, we have to look at what's happening mechanically.

Babies with reflux have an immature lower esophageal sphincter (the muscle that keeps stomach contents down). When you place a baby flat on their tummy, gravity is no longer helping keep that milk down. Worse, the floor puts direct upward pressure on their stomach. It’s exactly like eating a huge meal and then immediately lying stomach-down on a hard floor. It’s uncomfortable, it causes the milk to travel back up their esophagus (which burns!), and it naturally makes them want to cry.

## Why Reflux Babies Actually *Need* Tummy Time

It’s tempting to just skip tummy time until they outgrow the reflux. But developmentally, [tummy time](/#what-we-treat) is actually a crucial piece of the puzzle to *help* their reflux long-term.

Here is the secret: **core strength aids digestion.**

Tummy time improves overall strength in the core, shoulders, and neck. When a baby builds strength in their trunk, their digestive organs are better supported. They sit up earlier and with better posture, which allows gravity to assist digestion throughout the day. So while it feels counterintuitive, building that core and neck strength (which we talked about extensively in our post on [The Importance of Tummy Time](/posts/importance-of-tummy-time/)) is one of the best things you can do for their little digestive system.

## 4 Modified Tummy Time Positions for Reflux

The goal is to get the benefits of tummy time (neck extension, shoulder strengthening, taking weight through the arms) *without* putting direct pressure on a full, uncomfortable stomach.

Here are four OT-approved ways to modify tummy time for your reflux baby:

### 1. The Incline (Chest-to-Chest)
This is often the most comfortable position for babies with severe reflux. Recline yourself on the couch or bed at a 45-degree angle (use pillows to support your back). Lay your baby on your chest, facing you.
* **Why it works:** Your baby is still working against gravity to lift their head to look at your face, but the incline keeps their stomach contents down. Plus, the warmth of your body and the sound of your heartbeat are incredibly regulating, which helps calm their nervous system.

### 2. Over the Boppy (or a Rolled Towel)
Place a Boppy pillow or a firmly rolled towel under your baby’s chest, with their arms draped over the front.
* **Why it works:** This elevates their chest and head, reducing the severity of the reflux. More importantly, it takes a lot of the direct pressure off the center of their stomach, shifting the weight-bearing to their arms and lower pelvis.

### 3. The Football Hold (Tummy-Down Carry)
Carry your baby face down, with their chest resting on your forearm. Support their chest and neck with your hand, and let their arms and legs dangle.
* **Why it works:** You can carry them at a slight angle (head higher than their hips). They are still working those neck and back extensors, but the pressure is dispersed along your arm rather than compressed into the floor. This is a great way to sneak in 3-5 minutes of tummy time while walking around the house!

### 4. The Exercise Ball
If you have a yoga or exercise ball, lay a blanket over it and place your baby on top, gently holding them at their hips. Slowly rock the ball back and forth.
* **Why it works:** The curved surface of the ball conforms to their body, which is much softer than a flat floor. The movement is distracting and soothing, and you have complete control over the angle by how far back you roll the ball.

## Timing is Everything

When you have a baby with reflux, *when* you do tummy time is just as important as *how* you do it.

**Wait at least 30-45 minutes after a feed.** Doing tummy time immediately after eating is a recipe for disaster. Wait until the milk has had a chance to digest. For many babies, the "sweet spot" is right when they wake up from a nap, before they realize they are hungry again, or right before bath time.

If your baby starts showing signs of distress—arching their back, grimacing, or crying out in pain—stop. Tummy time should never be a battle of wills. Try one of the modifications above, or simply try again during their next wake window.
