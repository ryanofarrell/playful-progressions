Does your child seem to have two speeds: "off" and "tornado"? Do they crash into the couch, jump on the bed until the springs creak, or squeeze you so hard it hurts during a hug?

As a mom and an OT, I hear this all the time. "My child is just wild!" or "They have too much energy!" But often, what looks like "bad behavior" or hyperactivity is actually a sensory seek. Your child's body is hungry for input, and they are doing the only thing they know to get it: crashing, banging, and jumping.

The good news? There is a magic tool for this, and it’s called **Heavy Work**.

## Meet Proprioception: The Body's GPS

To understand heavy work, we first need to talk about **Proprioception**. This is often called our "sixth sense." It’s the internal sense that tells our brain where our body is in space. It comes from receptors in our muscles, joints, and tendons.

When you close your eyes and touch your nose, that’s proprioception. You don't need to *see* your arm to know where it is.

For some kids, this internal GPS is a little fuzzy. They don't quite feel "grounded" in their bodies. To compensate, they seek out intense input—crashing, jumping, stomping—to "wake up" those receptors and feel secure.

## Why Heavy Work Calms the Storm

Heavy work is any activity that pushes or pulls against resistance. When your child engages in heavy work, their muscles and joints send strong, clear signals to the brain. This input is generally **calming and organizing**.

Think about how you feel after a long hike, a yoga session, or even a tight hug. You feel grounded, right? That’s the power of proprioceptive input. It releases calming neurotransmitters (like serotonin and dopamine) that help regulate the nervous system.

If your child is "spinning out," heavy work can be the anchor that brings them back to earth.

## Easy Heavy Work Ideas for Home

You don't need fancy equipment to give your child a sensory diet of heavy work. Here are some of my favorite ways to incorporate it into daily life (and maybe get some chores done too!):

### 1. The Laundry Basket Push
This is a classic for a reason! Fill a laundry basket with books or toys (or another sibling!) and have your child push it across the floor. It’s an amazing workout for their core and provides deep input to their joints.
*Read more:* [5 Minute Friday - Laundry Baskets!]({% post_url 2024-12-06-five-minute-friday-laundry-baskets %})

### 2. Blanket Burritos & Rides
Wrap your child up tight in a blanket like a burrito (deep pressure!) or pull them around the house for a "sleigh ride." The friction of the blanket on the floor provides great resistance.
*Read more:* [5 Minute Friday - Blanket Rides!]({% post_url 2024-01-06-five-minute-friday-blanket-rides %})

### 3. Helping with Chores
Yes, really! Scrubbing the table, washing the car with a big sponge, or carrying grocery bags are all functional heavy work tasks.
*Read more:* [5 Minute Friday - Sponges!]({% post_url 2025-06-06-five-minute-friday-sponges %})

### 4. Animal Walks
Have a "commute" to the bathtub or dinner table.
- **Bear Walk:** Walk on hands and feet (butt in the air).
- **Crab Walk:** Walk on hands and feet with tummy facing up.
- **Frog Jump:** Squat down and jump forward.

### 5. Wall Pushes
This is great for a quick reset in a public place. Have your child place their hands on the wall and try to "push the room bigger" or "push the building over" for 10 seconds. It engages the shoulders and arms quickly.

## When to Use Heavy Work

The beauty of heavy work is that you can use it proactively.
- **Before** a transition (like leaving the house or getting into the car).
- **Before** a task that requires focus (like homework or dinner).
- **During** the "witching hour" when you see the energy levels spiking.

Remember, every child is unique. Watch your child's cues. If they seem happier and more settled after these activities, you know you've found a tool that works.

Parenting a high-energy child is exhausting, but understanding *why* they crash can change your perspective from frustration to fascination. You've got this, momma!
