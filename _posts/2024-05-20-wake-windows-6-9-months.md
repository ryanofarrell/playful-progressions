Welcome to the "Mover and Shaker" stage! If you thought the 3-6 month phase was fun, get ready, because between 6 and 9 months, your baby is practically a completely different person. This is the stage where so many huge milestones overlap: sitting up unsupported, starting solid foods, beginning to transition into crawling, and even showing off those new fine motor skills.

Wake windows are getting a bit longer now (typically around 2 to 3 hours), which means you have more time to fill, but also more opportunities for fun. It can feel exhausting to keep up with a baby who suddenly wants to be on the move, but remember to give yourself some grace—you're doing a great job.

Here are some of my favorite OT-mom approved ways to fill wake windows for your 6-9 month old!

## 1. Practice Unsupported Sitting
Around 6 months, many babies are learning to sit up all on their own. This requires a ton of core strength! To help them practice, place your baby in a seated position on the floor, surrounded by a "boppy" pillow or regular pillows just in case they topple over. Present toys at their midline (right in front of them) and encourage them to reach with both hands.

As they get stronger, start placing toys just slightly out of reach on either side. This encourages them to rotate their trunk, shift their weight, and eventually use their arms to catch themselves (what we call "protective extension").

## 2. Introduce Solid Foods (and Messy Play!)
Starting solids is a massive developmental leap. Whether you choose [baby-led weaning, purees, or a combined feeding model](/posts/combined-feeding-model/), mealtime is a multi-sensory experience.

Allowing your baby to get messy is actually incredible for their tactile development! Let them squish avocado between their fingers or smear yogurt on their high chair tray. Exploring the texture of food with their hands helps their brain understand what the food will feel like in their mouth, which can reduce picky eating later on.

## 3. Container Play (In and Out)
Right around 8-9 months, babies become fascinated with the concept of object permanence and spatial awareness. Grab a simple container—a Tupperware, an empty tissue box, or a clean laundry basket—and some blocks or medium-sized toys.

Show your baby how to drop the toys into the container and dump them back out. This is a foundational skill for fine motor development, as it requires them to voluntarily release an object (which is harder than grabbing it!). You'll be amazed at how long this simple game can keep a baby entertained.

## 4. Crawling Prep and Tunnels
As babies prepare to crawl, they need to build strength in their shoulders, core, and hips. Encourage pivoting on their belly by placing high-interest toys just out of reach in a circle around them during tummy time.

You can also create "tunnels" using a pop-up play tunnel or simply draping a blanket over two chairs. Getting on the floor with them and peeking through the other side of the tunnel is highly motivating! Even if they are just army crawling or scooting, this movement is fantastic for cross-lateral brain development.

## 5. Pincer Grasp Practice
As we approach 9 months, you'll start noticing your baby using their thumb and pointer finger to pick up smaller items—this is the famous pincer grasp!

You can encourage this during mealtime with safe, small foods like Puffs or O's cereal. Outside of mealtime, you can place small, baby-safe items (like pompoms, if supervised closely to prevent choking, or pulling tissues out of a box) in front of them. This precise fine motor skill is the foundation for holding a pencil, buttoning a shirt, and tying shoes down the road!

## 6. Read and Point
Reading is always a great way to wind down at the end of a wake window. At this age, babies are becoming more interactive with books. Choose sturdy board books with flaps to lift or textures to feel.

Start pointing to pictures as you name them. Pointing is a crucial pre-linguistic skill, and by modeling it, you're helping your baby learn how to communicate their interests.

## Keep it Simple
Remember, you do not need expensive toys to fill these wake windows. Your baby's favorite toy is *you*! Getting down on the floor, making silly faces, and narrating your day are some of the most beneficial things you can do for their development.

If you ever feel like your baby is struggling to meet motor milestones like sitting or transitioning into crawling, don't hesitate to reach out. An evaluation by a pediatric OT or PT can make a world of difference!
