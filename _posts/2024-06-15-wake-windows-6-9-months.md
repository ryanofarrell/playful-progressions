The 6-9 month window is such a fun and dynamic time! Your baby is likely becoming much more mobile, interactive, and curious about the world around them. As an occupational therapist and a mom, I know firsthand how quickly things change during these months. Babies go from needing a lot of support to suddenly rolling everywhere, attempting to sit up, and maybe even starting to crawl!

Whether you are in **Atlanta**, **Buckhead**, **Brookhaven**, or the surrounding [neighborhoods](/service-areas/), finding new ways to entertain your little explorer can feel like a full-time job. Here are some of my favorite ways to fill those wake windows and encourage development during the 6-9 month phase.

## 1. Sitting Play

At this stage, your baby is likely working on sitting independently or already has it down. Sitting is a major milestone because it frees up their hands to play and explore in a whole new way.

To encourage independent sitting, place toys just out of reach to encourage them to twist and reach, which strengthens their core. You can use a nursing pillow or regular pillows behind them for safety. Once they are sitting well, introduce toys that require two hands, like blocks to bang together or a simple shape sorter. This encourages them to use both sides of their body, a foundational skill for later tasks.

## 2. Crawling Obstacle Courses

Crawling is fantastic for developing [motor planning](/services/#treatments) and strengthening the shoulders and core. If your baby is showing signs of wanting to crawl (getting on hands and knees, rocking back and forth), you can set up simple obstacle courses to encourage movement.

Use pillows, couch cushions, or rolled-up blankets to create small hurdles for them to climb over. Place a highly desirable toy just out of reach to motivate them. If they aren't crawling yet, you can still encourage pivoting on their tummy by placing toys in a circle around them.

## 3. Fine Motor Refinement

Around this time, your baby is transitioning from a "raking" grasp (using all their fingers to scoop up an object) to a more precise "pincer" grasp (using the thumb and index finger). This is a crucial step for self-feeding.

To help them practice, offer safe, age-appropriate finger foods (if you've started solids and your pediatrician approves). You can also use toys with small parts to manipulate (always supervised!), like pushing buttons, turning dials, or pulling tissues out of an empty box.

## 4. Sensory Bins

Sensory play is essential for developing [tactile processing](/services/#treatments). It helps your baby learn about different textures, temperatures, and properties of objects.

Create simple, taste-safe sensory bins. A shallow tray filled with water and some floating toys is always a hit (and great for outside time!). You can also try plain yogurt (you can even add food coloring for fun), mashed potatoes, or even just a bin of large, safe, soft blocks. Remember, messy play is good play! It helps them build [body awareness](/services/#treatments) and learn how to interact with different materials.

## 5. Cause and Effect Toys

At 6-9 months, babies begin to understand cause and effect—"If I do this, that happens." This is a huge cognitive leap!

Toys that pop up, make noise when shaken, or light up when a button is pressed are perfect for this stage. You don't necessarily need expensive toys; a simple homemade shaker (a sealed container with some dried beans or rice inside) works just as well.

## 6. Supported Standing

If your baby is showing interest in standing, you can offer them opportunities for supported standing. This helps strengthen their leg muscles and prepares them for cruising and walking.

You can hold them securely under their arms while they bear weight on their legs, or let them hold onto a sturdy piece of furniture (like a low couch) while you stay right behind them for safety.

Enjoy this exciting phase! It's a time of rapid growth and discovery, and every little interaction helps build the foundation for their future skills.
